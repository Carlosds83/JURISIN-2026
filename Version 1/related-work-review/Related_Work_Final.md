# Related Work

The detection of exploitable ambiguities and interpretive flexibility in legal texts represents a critical challenge at the intersection of natural language processing, legal informatics, and computational compliance. This review organizes prior work into three thematic groups that collectively inform our approach to detecting "gray area triggers" in tax law: (1) research on NLP approaches to ambiguity detection in legal and technical domains, (2) language-based computational approaches specifically targeting tax uncertainty and loopholes, and (3) alternative computational methods for tax norm abuse detection using genetic algorithms. We critically examine how these studies relate to our research on combining expert annotations of gray-area properties with embedding-based classification to identify exploitable provisions in tax legislation.

## 1. Research on NLP, Ambiguities, Detection, and Law

### 1.1 Foundational Work on Ambiguity and Semantic Understanding

**Word Sense Disambiguation as a Baseline Framework**

Navigli (2009) provides a comprehensive taxonomy of approaches to Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD), organizing methodologies into supervised (machine learning on labeled data), unsupervised (clustering-based sense induction), and knowledge-based methods (exploiting dictionaries, thesauri, and ontologies). The survey establishes critical performance benchmarks: fine-grained all-words WSD achieves only 65-70% accuracy with WordNet, while coarse-grained sense inventories yield 78-83% accuracy. Notably, most systems fail to exceed the first sense baseline (most frequent sense) in all-words tasks, and supervised methods face a fundamental knowledge acquisition bottleneck requiring repetition across domains and languages.

For our research on tax law gray areas, Navigli's findings are particularly relevant in three ways. First, the observation that disambiguation performance depends critically on sense granularity directly parallels challenges in tax law interpretation, where the appropriate level of semantic distinction varies by application context—similar to our five trigger categories (Relevance, Completeness, Discretionality, Differential Regime, Interpretability) that operationalize different dimensions of interpretive flexibility. Second, the knowledge acquisition bottleneck that constrains supervised WSD motivates our use of expert annotations to capture domain-specific legal knowledge that general-purpose language models may miss. Third, the finding that fine-grained disambiguation achieves only 65-70% accuracy suggests that detecting subtle interpretive properties in legal text—our gray-area triggers—represents a genuinely difficult computational challenge requiring specialized approaches.

However, traditional WSD assumes that sense inventories exist and aims to select the correct sense for each occurrence. Our task differs fundamentally: we seek to identify when legal text exhibits properties (ambiguity, incompleteness, discretionality) that enable multiple defensible interpretations—not to resolve which interpretation is correct, but to detect that exploitable flexibility exists. This distinction motivates our classification approach using expert annotations rather than attempting full semantic disambiguation.

**Probing What Language Models Encode About Ambiguity**

Bhattacharya et al. (2022) employ probing classifiers—shallow MLPs trained on frozen representations from BERT and GPT-2—to test whether pre-trained language models encode linguistic traits including complexity, grammaticality, and ambiguity. Using datasets including 922 ambiguous sentences from MS COCO, they reveal critical methodological issues: ambiguity is poorly encoded in pre-trained models, with performance barely exceeding TF-IDF baselines. Their finding that middle layers outperform upper layers for all traits suggests information loss at higher abstraction levels. Crucially, they demonstrate that surface-level lexical features can "solve" tasks designed to probe semantic understanding, raising questions about whether models capture genuine semantic properties or merely correlate with superficial patterns.

This work directly motivates our research design in two key ways. First, Bhattacharya et al.'s finding that ambiguity is weakly encoded in general-purpose language models supports our use of domain-specific models (Legal-BERT, MEL) rather than relying on generic pre-trained representations. Second, their emphasis on rigorous baselines and the concern about distinguishing lexical from semantic encoding directly informs our inclusion of TF-IDF as a baseline and our sensitivity analysis using synonym replacement and invariance tests. Where they probe frozen representations on general linguistic ambiguity, we fine-tune embeddings via logistic regression on domain-specific legal concepts, and our sensitivity analysis explicitly addresses whether embeddings capture semantic gray-area properties or merely lexical patterns associated with exploitable provisions.

**Semantic Underspecification in Language Models**

Wildenburg et al. (2024) introduce DUST (Dataset of Underspecified Sentences by Type), containing 2,123 English underspecified sentences with specified counterparts, to assess whether language models recognize varying degrees of semantic underspecification. Using perplexity-based evaluation rather than unreliable generative prompting, they find that models recognize underspecification at rates above chance but exhibit little uncertainty when interpreting underspecified sentences—contrary to theoretical predictions. Performance improves with concrete words, suggesting lexical features may confound semantic understanding. Critically, models fail to recognize when underspecified sentences license continuations their specified counterparts do not.

Wildenburg's focus on semantic underspecification—one form of open-texture—closely aligns with our gray-area triggers, particularly "Interpretability" and "Completeness." Their finding that models struggle with underspecification despite surface-level competence echoes concerns about whether embeddings capture deep semantic properties relevant to legal exploitation. The correlation with concreteness suggests lexical features may confound semantic understanding—precisely what our synonym replacement tests aim to disentangle. However, our approaches differ fundamentally: Wildenburg evaluates underspecification recognition (metalinguistic judgment), whereas we classify presence of gray-area properties via supervised learning. Their perplexity-based method avoids annotation subjectivity but cannot leverage expert domain knowledge. Our approach inverts this trade-off: we accept annotation variability but gain domain-specific legal expertise that generic language understanding cannot provide.

### 1.2 Ambiguity Detection in Legal and Technical Domains

**Open-Texture Annotation in Legal Texts**

Guitton et al. (2025a) develop a multi-stage human annotation protocol for identifying open-texture in legal texts, using a four-question framework to guide annotators: (1) multiple quantities, (2) broad spectrum of meanings, (3) no agreed definition, (4) value-laden terms. Annotators mark open-textured words and assign an Open-Texture Value on a 1-5 scale. Applying this methodology to four EU legal texts (GDPR, LED, EU Charter, ECHR) with 26 participants, they achieve high agreement (Cohen's kappa averaged 0.70 after reconciliation, up from 0.24 in Round 1). Critically, they find that most clauses contain open-texture, and Question 4 (value-laden terms) strongly associates with high Open-Texture Values.

This work directly parallels our annotation challenges and methodological choices. The low initial agreement (0.24) followed by substantial improvement (0.70) through structured protocols mirrors challenges in annotating gray-area triggers and motivates our use of expert tax law annotators with structured trigger definitions. Guitton's four-question framework resembles our five trigger categories as operationalized definitions of abstract legal concepts. The finding that open-texture is pervasive suggests gray-area triggers may also be widespread in tax law, supporting our annotation-intensive approach. However, critical differences exist: Guitton focuses on text-level, word-by-word open-texture with intensity ratings (1-5 scale), whereas we classify article-level presence of gray-area triggers using binary or ordinal categories. Both approaches acknowledge that no single ground truth exists, but Guitton uses inter-annotator agreement as the metric, while we use expert consensus to capture domain-specific knowledge about which provisions enable exploitation.

**Cross-Domain Ambiguity Detection Using Word Embeddings**

Ferrari and Esuli (2019) use word2vec skip-gram models trained on domain-specific Wikipedia corpora to identify ambiguous terms across domains (Computer Science, Engineering, Medicine, Sports). They compute ambiguity scores by comparing lists of most similar words across domain-specific embeddings, using a rank-weighted sum of variance in similarity values. Evaluating across seven requirements elicitation scenarios with Amazon Mechanical Turk annotators, they achieve Kendall's τ up to 0.88 for two-domain interviews but performance degrades for multi-domain meetings (0.36). False positives arise from non-representative Wikipedia samples, high-level abstract concepts that don't vary by domain, and domain-specific usage missed by non-expert annotators.

Ferrari's domain-specific embeddings approach directly parallels our use of Legal-BERT (domain-adapted) versus generic embeddings. Their finding that domain-specific representations improve ambiguity detection motivates domain-specific corpora—analogous to Legal-BERT's training on legal texts. However, key differences distinguish our work: Ferrari targets cross-domain ambiguity (same word, different meanings across domains), whereas we target intra-domain gray areas (legal interpretive flexibility within tax law). Their unsupervised ranking contrasts with our supervised classification using expert annotations. Critically, their evaluation reveals annotator limitations: non-experts miss domain-specific nuances and misinterpret instructions. This underscores our choice of expert annotators for tax law, where recognizing exploitable provisions requires specialized legal knowledge that crowdworkers cannot provide.

**Domain-Specific Corpus Generation for Requirements Ambiguity**

Ezzini et al. (2021) propose an automated pipeline for handling coordination ambiguity (CA) and prepositional-phrase attachment ambiguity (PAA) in requirements engineering. Their approach combines structural patterns to detect ambiguous constructions, corpus-based heuristics leveraging semantic and morphological information, and domain-specific corpus generation from Wikipedia. Applying this to 20 industrial requirements documents (5,156 requirements) with ground truth from linguistics experts, they achieve ~80% precision and ~89% recall overall. Critically, domain-specific corpora improve detection by ~33% and interpretation by ~16% over generic corpora.

Ezzini's finding that domain-specific corpora substantially improve accuracy directly supports our hypothesis that legal domain adaptation matters, motivating our comparison of TF-IDF, Legal-BERT, and MEL. The distinction between acknowledged and unacknowledged ambiguity (where stakeholders disagree on interpretation) resonates with our focus on "gray areas"—zones where interpretive disagreement is possible but not immediately obvious. However, our approaches differ fundamentally: Ezzini targets structural ambiguity types (CA, PAA) using rule-based patterns with corpus support, whereas we target conceptual gray-area triggers using embedding-based supervised learning. They generate interpretations; we classify trigger presence. Their error analysis reveals abbreviation and NLP pipeline vulnerabilities—issues our sensitivity analysis (synonym replacement, invariance tests) implicitly tests by examining robustness to lexical variation.

**Task-Specific Embeddings for Ambiguity Detection**

Kim et al. (2023) develop task-specific embeddings extracted from trained classification models to detect ambiguity in smart speaker commands. Creating three embedding types from intermediate layers (Whole Layer Vector, After Representation Layer Vector, Conclusion Layer Vector) using custom CNN and KoGPT2-based classifiers, they evaluate on 2.2 million training sentences and 29,136 ambiguity test sentences. Task-specific embeddings outperform off-the-shelf embeddings (Sent2Vec, KoGPT2), with significant positive correlation with user-perceived class relevance. Notably, ordinary users exhibit higher similarity than experts; experts focus on service area distinctions while ordinary users weight predicates equally.

Kim's task-specific embeddings parallel our use of fine-tuned embeddings adapted to legal domain. Their finding that task-adapted representations outperform generic ones supports domain-specific embedding approaches. The distinction between expert and ordinary user judgments echoes challenges in legal annotation: experts may focus on technical distinctions (like our gray-area triggers) that non-experts miss. However, key differences exist: Kim targets command ambiguity in short, imperative Korean sentences, whereas we target legal concept ambiguity in tax law articles. Their embeddings are extracted from trained classifiers, while ours are pre-trained then fine-tuned via logistic regression. They use unsupervised similarity to detect ambiguity, while we use supervised classification with expert annotations. Their language-specific limitation highlights a gap: our approach should be tested across languages to assess generalizability.

### 1.3 Synthesis: NLP Approaches to Ambiguity in Legal Contexts

Collectively, these works establish several key insights that inform our research. First, **ambiguity is multi-faceted and domain-dependent**: it manifests as structural patterns (CA/PAA) in requirements engineering, semantic underspecification in general language, cross-domain polysemy in technical discussions, and legal open-texture in regulations. Tax law's "gray-area triggers" represent a distinct phenomenon—not structural ambiguity or polysemy, but interpretive discretion enabling norm abuse. Second, **domain-specific representations outperform generic ones**: Ezzini's domain-specific corpora improve detection by ~33%, Ferrari's domain-specific embeddings enable cross-domain ranking, and Kim's task-specific embeddings outperform off-the-shelf alternatives. This strongly supports our use of Legal-BERT and MEL over generic embeddings. Third, **annotation challenges are pervasive**: Guitton's 0.24 initial kappa, Bhattacharya's concerns about baseline-driven false positives, and Ferrari's findings that non-experts miss domain-specific nuances all motivate our use of expert tax law annotators with structured trigger definitions. Fourth, **distinguishing lexical from semantic understanding requires explicit testing**: Bhattacharya shows TF-IDF can "solve" semantic tasks, Wildenburg finds concreteness correlates with underspecification recognition, and Ferrari's false positives arise from surface features—all supporting our sensitivity analysis to disentangle lexical patterns from semantic understanding.

## 2. Language Approaches to Tax Uncertainty and Loopholes

### 2.1 LLM-Based Tax Strategy Generation and Evaluation

**LLM-Generated Novel Tax Strategies**

Blair-Stanek et al. (2025) use OpenAI's o1 reasoning model to generate tax minimization strategies, providing the model with background facts, goals, and relevant tax authorities. Building a dataset of 36 known tax strategies with structured components (facts, goals, authorities, steps, legal analysis), they demonstrate what appears to be the first publicly disclosed LLM-created tax strategy. The generated strategy involves straddles and common trust funds (section 584), using a "loss, dilute, gain" structure that differs from the original Notice 2003-54's "gain, dilute, loss" approach. The strategy exploits the fact that section 1092 only bars losses before gains in the same year, enabling multi-year exploitation. The authors predict advances in information retrieval will enable AI to find novel authority combinations more efficiently than human experts.

This demonstration that LLMs can generate novel tax strategies directly motivates our research on automated detection of tax norm abuse: if AI can create strategies, it should potentially identify them. Their structured dataset format (facts, goals, authorities, steps, analysis) parallels our structured annotation of gray-area triggers. The finding that o1's reasoning capability enables strategy generation suggests advanced language models may capture legal reasoning patterns—relevant to whether embeddings encode gray-area properties. However, critical differences distinguish our work: Blair-Stanek focuses on strategy generation (offensive capability), whereas we focus on gray-area detection (defensive capability). They use generative reasoning models (o1), while we use discriminative classification (logistic regression on embeddings). Their dataset is structured tax strategies, while ours is raw tax law articles with expert annotations. They demonstrate LLM capability but don't evaluate detection or classification—the inverse of our task, highlighting the arms race where detection methods become crucial as AI generates strategies.

**Evaluating LLM Reasoning on Tax Strategies**

Blair-Stanek et al. (2026) introduce Shelter Check, a dataset for evaluating LLM reasoning on U.S. tax strategies. Testing three reasoning models (OpenAI's o3, Anthropic's Claude-4, Google's Gemini-2.5) and one baseline (Meta's Llama-3.3-Instruct 70B) across 36 tax strategies, they evaluate four tasks: goal verification (does the strategy meet its goals?), adversarial verification (testing with incorrect steps), step-cloze (filling in missing steps), and generation from scratch. o3 and Claude-4 find strategies viable at high rates with analysis provided, but performance drops without analysis. Spearman's ρ for whole-strategy grading is only 0.30-0.48 (versus 0.89 for step-cloze), indicating whole-strategy grading is much harder. Notably, o1-preview generated an apparently novel strategy rated 3/5 by experts.

This multi-task evaluation framework provides a model for assessing LLM capabilities on complex legal reasoning. The finding that analysis improves performance suggests that explanatory annotations (like our trigger definitions) may enhance model understanding. The low agreement between LLM and expert grades (ρ=0.30-0.48) parallels challenges in automated gray-area detection—legal reasoning is difficult to capture computationally. However, key differences exist: Blair-Stanek evaluates LLM reasoning capabilities (verification, generation), whereas we evaluate embedding-based classification of gray-area properties. They use reasoning models (o3, Claude-4), while we use encoder models (Legal-BERT, MEL). Their tasks require multi-step legal reasoning, while ours requires property recognition (does this article exhibit Discretionality?). Their low inter-model agreement and poor whole-strategy grading suggest that even advanced LLMs struggle with holistic legal assessment—supporting our focus on narrower, expert-annotated trigger classification rather than end-to-end reasoning.

### 2.2 Computational Detection of Open-Texture and Tax Loopholes

**Automated Open-Texture Detection Using LLMs**

Guitton et al. (2025b) test whether LLMs can automatically detect open-texture in legal texts, comparing gpt-3.5-turbo (proprietary) and llama-2-70b-chat (open-source) on the GDPR with 12 human annotators. They test six different prompts varying by inclusion of guiding questions and few-shot examples. gpt-3.5-turbo achieves F₁=0.84 (precision=0.85, recall=0.84), while llama-2-70b-chat achieves F₁=0.67 (precision=0.53, recall=0.92). Adding guiding questions improves accuracy by 5 percentage points; few-shot examples improve by 8 percentage points. However, only 42% of terms flagged by human annotators in prior work appear in gpt-3.5-turbo output (24% for llama), and inter-annotator agreement is low (Cohen's kappa: 0.01-0.36, average=0.15).

This finding that LLMs can detect open-texture with high F₁ suggests they may also detect gray-area triggers, though the low inter-annotator agreement parallels challenges in annotating abstract legal concepts like Discretionality or Interpretability. The result that prompt engineering matters (guiding questions +5%, few-shot +8%) informs how we might use LLMs to augment annotations. However, the finding that gpt-3.5-turbo identifies different terms than human annotators (only 42% overlap) raises questions about whether LLMs capture the same concepts—relevant to our comparison of TF-IDF, Legal-BERT, and MEL. Critical differences distinguish our work: Guitton evaluates LLM-generated annotations against human ground truth, whereas we evaluate embedding-based classifiers trained on human annotations. They use generative LLMs (gpt-3.5-turbo, llama-2-70b-chat), while we use encoder embeddings (Legal-BERT, MEL). They detect open-texture (vagueness, ambiguity), while we detect gray-area triggers (abuse-enabling properties). Their focus on term-level detection contrasts with our article-level classification. The low human agreement and divergence between LLM and human outputs underscore the subjectivity of legal vagueness—a challenge our expert annotation protocol must address.

**AI for Tax Loophole Identification via Planning**

Fratrič et al. (2025) propose a hybrid neuro-symbolic system combining LLMs with formal planning to expose tax loopholes. The system uses: (1) LLM as natural language interface to translate legal language into a domain-specific language, (2) Prolog-based transition system modeling states (corporate structure, IP ownership) and actions (addChild, rentIP, transferIP) with preconditions defined by legal rules, (3) tax reduction formalization as two-step process, and (4) randomized best-first search to explore tax planning trajectories. Applying this to multinational corporate structures (e.g., "Double Irish with Dutch Sandwich"), they successfully expose tax loopholes, with utility profiles showing non-constant structure indicating some strategies are significantly more profitable. Certain tax provisions are exploited disproportionately in high-utility segments. However, the main bottleneck is LLM formalization of legal rules, which is insufficient; fine-tuning on human-formalized rules is needed.

Fratrič's hybrid approach (LLM + symbolic reasoning) offers an alternative to pure embedding-based methods. Their finding that LLMs struggle with legal formalization supports our use of expert annotations rather than relying on LLM interpretation alone. The identification of disproportionately exploited provisions parallels our goal of detecting gray-area triggers that enable abuse. However, key differences exist: Fratrič uses formal planning (Prolog transition systems), whereas we use statistical classification (logistic regression on embeddings). They require explicit legal rule formalization, which is resource-intensive; we rely on implicit patterns learned from annotations. Their approach is generative (finding profitable strategies), while ours is discriminative (classifying gray-area properties). Critically, their system identifies loopholes through frequency analysis of rule usage—an orthogonal signal to our trigger-based approach. Their theoretical grounding (Theorem 2: identifying underpaying strategies can increase social welfare) provides justification for why detection matters; our empirical approach complements this by operationalizing detection.

### 2.3 Synthesis: Language-Based Tax Compliance Research

These works collectively demonstrate both the promise and limitations of language-based approaches to tax compliance. **LLMs show capability but significant limitations**: Blair-Stanek demonstrates LLMs can generate novel tax strategies but also shows poor agreement with expert grades (ρ=0.30-0.48). Guitton finds gpt-3.5-turbo detects open-texture with F₁=0.84 but identifies different terms than humans (only 42% overlap). Fratrič shows LLMs struggle with legal formalization. These mixed results suggest LLMs may augment but not replace expert-driven approaches. **Strategy generation motivates detection**: If AI can create exploitation strategies, automated detection becomes crucial—positioning our gray-area trigger classification as a defensive complement to generative capabilities. **Hybrid approaches offer advantages**: Fratrič's combination of LLM translation with symbolic reasoning suggests that pure embedding-based methods may benefit from structured legal knowledge, though our approach of learning from expert annotations provides an alternative path to incorporating domain expertise.

Critical gaps remain that our research addresses. First, no prior work specifically classifies abuse-enabling properties in tax law text using expert annotations—Blair-Stanek evaluates strategy generation/verification, Fratrič identifies loopholes through planning, and Guitton detects general open-texture, but none operationalize specific triggers like Relevance, Completeness, Discretionality, Differential Regime, and Interpretability. Second, none systematically compare embedding approaches (TF-IDF, Legal-BERT, MEL) with sensitivity analysis to distinguish lexical from semantic understanding—a methodological contribution our research provides. Third, the low LLM-expert agreement in Blair-Stanek's work and the divergence between LLM and human annotations in Guitton's work highlight the need for approaches that learn from expert judgment rather than relying on LLM interpretation alone.

## 3. Other Approaches to Tax Norm Abuse: Genetic Algorithms

### 3.1 Co-evolutionary Detection of Tax Evasion

**Modeling Adversarial Taxpayer-Auditor Dynamics**

Hemberg et al. (2016) employ competitive co-evolutionary genetic algorithms to model the adversarial dynamics between tax evaders and auditors, drawing parallels to biological co-evolution where species reciprocally affect each other's evolution. Their STEALTH framework represents the tax ecosystem through three components: tax entities (taxpayers, partnerships) as nodes with assets, ownership networks where edges represent ownership relations, and transactions as paired actions transferring assets between entities. The system implements the "Red Queen Principle"—the biological observation that species must continually adapt to maintain relative fitness—manifested as tax evaders seeking to maximize deductible loss while minimizing audit likelihood, and auditors seeking to maximize audit likelihood for high-loss transaction sequences.

The genetic algorithm evolves two populations simultaneously: a tax scheme population where individuals represent transaction sequences designed to generate deductible losses, and an audit score sheet population where individuals represent detection rules assigning audit points to suspicious transaction patterns. Experimental results demonstrate sustained oscillatory dynamics: initial high fitness for tax schemes is rapidly suppressed by evolved audit score sheets, followed by emergence of new undetectable schemes generating rapid upward surge in tax scheme fitness, then gradual audit evolution to detect new dominant schemes. The experiments successfully model the Installment Sale Bogus Optional Basis (iBOB) scheme, evolving variants like HiBOB1 that achieve similar tax outcomes through modified transaction sequences.

**Contrasts with Language-Based Methods**

This approach differs fundamentally from NLP-based methods in several critical ways. First, **rule-based simulation versus text analysis**: STEALTH does not process natural language or extract semantic information from statutory text. Instead, it directly encodes tax law rules as computational constraints within a graph-based transaction simulator. This contrasts sharply with NLP approaches that must first extract meaning from legal language before applying it. Second, **structural versus linguistic representation**: the graph-based formalism (nodes as entities, edges as ownership, transactions as state transitions) represents tax relationships structurally rather than linguistically, enabling reasoning about transactional mechanics without confronting linguistic ambiguity—a core challenge for language-based methods. Third, **search-based versus learning-based**: STEALTH employs evolutionary search through the space of possible transaction sequences, whereas supervised NLP methods learn patterns from labeled examples. This enables STEALTH to anticipate novel schemes rather than merely recognizing known patterns. Fourth, **data requirements**: the approach explicitly avoids requiring taxpayer data, operating "similar to a minmax search in a game of chess," alleviating privacy concerns and the labeled data requirement that limits supervised detection to "already known cases of abuse."

### 3.2 Insights on Tax Compliance Arms Race

**Perpetual Adversarial Dynamics**

Hemberg et al.'s work reveals several critical insights about adversarial tax compliance. The Red Queen Principle formalizes the observation that "each time the IRS changes the tax code the tax evaders react by finding new ambiguities," suggesting that static detection approaches will inevitably become obsolete as adversaries adapt. The oscillatory dynamics demonstrate that neither side maintains permanent advantage: successful detection drives scheme innovation, while scheme proliferation drives detection evolution—a cycle that cannot be permanently resolved through any single intervention. The challenges enumerated—hierarchies up to 100 tiers with 100,000 partners, dispersed reporting data, intentional obfuscation—reveal that structural complexity itself serves as an evasion mechanism, suggesting that detection approaches must address both semantic ambiguity (language-based methods) and structural obfuscation (graph-based methods).

The framework's ability to "explore the likely forms of tax schemes in response to changes in audit policies" provides anticipatory capability—an "early warning system to help focus enforcement efforts." This anticipatory capacity distinguishes it from reactive approaches that respond only to observed violations. The observation of "jagged fitness landscape of the transaction sequences" indicates that the space of possible schemes contains numerous local optima, requiring sophisticated search to identify emerging strategies rather than simple pattern matching.

### 3.3 Complementarity and Integration with Language-Based Research

**Orthogonal Problem Spaces**

Hemberg et al.'s transactional simulation addresses structural complexity in entity networks and asset flows, while language-based approaches (including our work) address semantic ambiguity in legal text—determining what the law says and identifying exploitable interpretive flexibility. A comprehensive tax compliance system requires both: understanding what the law says (linguistic) and detecting how actors manipulate structures (transactional). Both approaches confront forms of ambiguity, though in different domains: WSD and our gray-area triggers address lexical and legal ambiguity where language carries multiple meanings, while STEALTH addresses regulatory ambiguity where transaction sequences exploit "new ambiguities" in tax code.

**Critical Gaps and Integration Opportunities**

Neither STEALTH nor traditional WSD directly addresses the problem of identifying ambiguous or exploitable language in regulatory text before observable schemes emerge. Navigli's WSD assumes sense inventories exist; Hemberg assumes rules are encoded. Our work using expert annotations to identify "gray area" triggers addresses this gap by capturing expert judgment about interpretive difficulty and ambiguity, identifying linguistic features associated with exploitability, and building predictive models for ambiguity from text alone. STEALTH operates entirely at the behavioral level, encoding rules but not analyzing the linguistic properties that make rules exploitable. Our annotation-based approach bridges this gap by linking linguistic features to exploitation potential.

Integration opportunities exist across multiple dimensions. Language-informed simulation could combine NLP analysis of statutory text with transactional simulation: use disambiguation techniques to identify ambiguous terms in tax code, extract selectional preferences to define valid transaction constraints, employ domain-specific disambiguation to resolve technical tax terminology, and feed disambiguated rules into STEALTH-like simulation to predict exploitation patterns. Embedding-based pattern detection could enhance transactional analysis by representing transaction types in semantic space based on their descriptions in guidance documents, identifying analogous schemes across different regulatory domains through embedding similarity, and detecting when new guidance language creates semantic shifts that enable novel interpretations. A co-evolutionary language-behavior model could unify evolution of statutory language (regulatory side), evolution of interpretive arguments (taxpayer side), evolution of transaction patterns (behavioral side), and co-evolution of all three in adversarial interaction.

## Positioning the Current Research

### Key Patterns Across Literature

The reviewed work establishes several foundational insights. First, **ambiguity is multi-faceted and domain-dependent**, manifesting differently across contexts: structural (CA/PAA) in requirements engineering, semantic underspecification in general language, cross-domain polysemy in technical discussions, legal open-texture in regulations, and transactional exploitation in tax schemes. Tax law's "gray-area triggers" represent a distinct phenomenon—not structural ambiguity or polysemy, but interpretive discretion enabling norm abuse. Second, **domain-specific representations outperform generic ones**: Ezzini's domain-specific corpora improve ambiguity detection by ~33%, Ferrari's domain-specific embeddings enable cross-domain ranking, Kim's task-specific embeddings outperform off-the-shelf alternatives, and Guitton shows LLMs can detect open-texture with F₁=0.84 when properly prompted. This strongly supports our use of Legal-BERT and MEL over generic embeddings. Third, **annotation challenges are pervasive**: Guitton's 0.01-0.36 kappa for open-texture, Bhattacharya's concerns about baseline-driven false positives, and Ferrari's findings that non-experts miss domain-specific nuances all motivate our use of expert tax law annotators with structured trigger definitions. Fourth, **LLMs show promise but have significant limitations**: Blair-Stanek demonstrates LLMs can generate novel tax strategies but shows poor agreement with expert grades (ρ=0.30-0.48), Guitton finds gpt-3.5-turbo detects open-texture but identifies different terms than humans (only 42% overlap), and Fratrič shows LLMs struggle with legal formalization. These mixed results suggest LLMs may augment but not replace expert-driven approaches.

### Critical Gaps Addressed

Our research addresses five critical gaps in the existing literature:

**1. Tax Law as a Distinct Domain**: No prior work specifically targets tax norm abuse detection using NLP on legal text. Blair-Stanek evaluates strategy generation/verification, Fratrič identifies loopholes through planning, but neither classifies abuse-enabling properties in legal text. Requirements ambiguity, legal open-texture, and cross-domain polysemy are related but distinct phenomena. Our focus on tax law gray-area triggers provides the first linguistic approach specifically designed for this domain.

**2. Expert-Annotated Gray-Area Triggers**: While Guitton annotates open-texture and Ezzini distinguishes acknowledged/unacknowledged ambiguity, no work operationalizes specific abuse-enabling properties with expert tax law annotations. Our five triggers (Relevance, Completeness, Discretionality, Differential Regime, Interpretability) provide a structured taxonomy grounded in domain expertise, informed by consultation with tax law experts, reconstruction of avoidance schemes, and careful review of uncertainty principle literature.

**3. Embedding-Based Classification with Sensitivity Analysis**: Most work uses either rule-based methods (Ezzini), unsupervised ranking (Ferrari), or LLM generation (Blair-Stanek, Guitton). Our supervised classification using logistic regression on embeddings (TF-IDF, Legal-BERT, MEL) with sensitivity analysis (invariance tests, synonym replacement) directly tests whether embeddings capture semantic versus lexical properties—a question raised but not systematically addressed in prior work. Bhattacharya shows TF-IDF can "solve" semantic tasks, Wildenburg finds concreteness correlates with underspecification recognition, and Ferrari's false positives arise from surface features—all motivating our explicit examination of what embeddings encode.

**4. Bridging Lexical and Semantic Understanding**: Our synonym replacement and invariance tests explicitly disentangle lexical patterns from semantic understanding—a methodological contribution absent in prior work. While Bhattacharya raises concerns about surface-level features and Wildenburg finds lexical correlations, no work systematically tests robustness to meaning-preserving perturbations in the legal domain. This addresses the fundamental question: do embeddings capture genuine semantic properties of gray-area triggers, or merely correlate with superficial lexical patterns?

**5. Comparative Evaluation of Domain-Adapted Embeddings**: While Ezzini compares domain-specific versus generic corpora and Kim compares task-specific versus off-the-shelf embeddings, no work systematically compares TF-IDF, Legal-BERT, and MEL for legal concept classification in tax law. Our three-way comparison quantifies the value of domain adaptation (Legal-BERT trained on legal texts) and language-specific legal pre-training (MEL trained on Spanish legal texts), directly testing whether compute power and domain fine-tuning improve gray-area trigger classification.

**6. Complexity-Performance Relationship**: We examine the relationship between legal text complexity and classification performance—a dimension not explored in prior work. Given that law complexity relates to tax avoidance, we test whether more complex articles (potentially containing more relations, inducing more noise) result in worse performance, or whether complexity features and gray-area triggers are entangled such that models capture both simultaneously.

### Synthesis and Contribution

Our work synthesizes insights from multiple research streams: we adopt domain-specific embeddings (Group 1's lesson on domain adaptation) to address a tax law application (Group 2's domain), while incorporating methodological rigor from both linguistic research (Group 1's sensitivity to annotation quality and baseline comparison) and computational modeling (Group 3's recognition of adversarial dynamics). We move beyond LLM-based generation to embedding-based classification, addressing concerns about LLM reliability while retaining domain adaptation benefits. By combining expert annotations (addressing inter-annotator issues and capturing domain-specific knowledge) with sensitivity analysis (addressing lexical confounding), we provide a rigorous methodology for detecting gray-area triggers.

The reviewed literature establishes that: (1) domain adaptation matters, (2) annotation is challenging but essential, (3) LLMs are promising but insufficient alone, (4) distinguishing lexical from semantic understanding requires explicit testing, and (5) adversarial dynamics between regulators and avoiders create a perpetual arms race. Our research operationalizes these lessons in the novel context of tax norm abuse detection, contributing both a new dataset (expert-annotated gray-area triggers in tax law) and a methodological framework (embedding-based classification with sensitivity analysis and complexity examination) to an underexplored but socially critical domain. Where prior work addresses either general ambiguity detection or tax strategy generation/verification, we bridge these streams by identifying linguistic properties in legal text that enable exploitation—providing a defensive capability to complement offensive strategy generation and a linguistic foundation to inform behavioral modeling of tax avoidance.
